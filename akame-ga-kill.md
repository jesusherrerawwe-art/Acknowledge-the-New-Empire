# Akame ga Kill!

A research overview of the manga and anime series *Akame ga Kill!*, including its
story, characters, themes, and the differences between the manga and the anime
adaptation.

## 1. Overview

*Akame ga Kill!* (アカメが斬る!, *Akame ga Kiru!*) is a Japanese dark-fantasy
manga series written by **Takahiro** and illustrated by **Tetsuya Tashiro**. It
was serialized in Square Enix's *Monthly Gangan Joker* from **March 2010 to
December 2016**, with its chapters collected in **15 tankōbon volumes**.

A 24-episode anime television adaptation, produced by **White Fox** and directed
by **Tomoki Kobayashi**, aired in Japan from **July to December 2014**. The
anime is well known for diverging from the manga in its second half, since the
manga had not yet finished when the show went into production.

A prequel manga, ***Akame ga Kill! Zero***, illustrated by Kei Toru, ran from
October 2013 to January 2019 and focuses on Akame's past as an Imperial
assassin before her defection.

## 2. Setting

The story takes place in an unnamed Empire roughly 1,000 years after a series
of catastrophic wars. The Empire was once a great nation, but is now in rapid
decline:

- The current **Emperor is a child**, easily manipulated.
- The true power behind the throne is **Prime Minister Honest**, a corrupt,
  gluttonous schemer who uses the Emperor as a puppet while looting the
  treasury, enabling the nobility, and crushing dissent.
- The countryside is plagued by poverty, banditry, and **Danger Beasts**
  (powerful mutated creatures left over from ancient times).
- A growing **Revolutionary Army** is preparing to overthrow the Empire from
  the southern territories.

The world is brutal, sometimes outright nihilistic, and the series makes a
point of showing that "good" characters die just as easily as "evil" ones.

## 3. Plot Summary

**Tatsumi**, a naive but skilled young swordsman, leaves his impoverished
mountain village with two childhood friends, Sayo and Ieyasu, hoping to earn
money in the Imperial Capital and send it home. Almost immediately he is
robbed of his savings by a charismatic woman named **Leone**, fails to enlist
in the army, and ends up on the streets.

A noble lady takes him in, seemingly out of kindness. He soon discovers that
this family — and many others in the Capital — abducts and tortures young
travelers for sport. His friends Sayo and Ieyasu are already dead. That night
the assassin group **Night Raid** raids the estate, and Leone reveals that
his "kind" hosts were her real target.

Confronted with the rot at the heart of the Empire, Tatsumi joins Night Raid,
which is the covert assassination wing of the Revolutionary Army. The rest of
the story follows their missions: eliminating corrupt nobles, military
officers, and, eventually, key figures around the Prime Minister, while a
specialized counter-force called the **Jaegers** is assembled to hunt them
down.

## 4. The Imperial Arms (Teigu)

A central piece of worldbuilding. About **1,000 years ago**, the first Emperor
commissioned **48 ultimate weapons** — the **Teigu** (translated in the anime
as *Imperial Arms*) — forged from the bones, organs, and blood of Danger
Beasts and rare materials, using lost alchemical techniques. Many have been
destroyed or lost over the centuries.

A Teigu can be:

- A weapon (sword, scissors, spear, sniper rifle).
- A piece of armor.
- A living creature, including biological "shapeshifter" beasts.
- A utilitarian object (e.g. a cosmetic case).

Teigu cannot be mass-produced, and most users die when their Teigu is broken
or when they push it past its limits. Almost every major fight in the series
is essentially a Teigu-versus-Teigu duel.

## 5. Major Factions and Characters

### 5.1 Night Raid (assassins of the Revolutionary Army)

| Character | Teigu | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Najenda** | *Infinite Uses: Susanoo* (a humanoid biological Teigu) | Leader. Former Imperial general who defected. One-eyed, one-armed. |
| **Akame** | *One-Cut Killer: Murasame* — a cursed katana whose smallest cut delivers a lethal poison curse. | The titular character. Stoic ex-Imperial assassin, defected after Najenda spared her. |
| **Tatsumi** | *Demon Armor: Incursio* (inherited from Bulat) | The protagonist; village boy turned revolutionary. |
| **Leone** | *Animal King: Lionel* — a transformation belt that grants beast-like strength and senses. | Streetwise brawler, the team's recruiter. |
| **Mine** | *Roman Artillery: Pumpkin* — a sniper rifle that grows in power as the user faces more danger. | Pink-haired sharpshooter; develops a relationship with Tatsumi. |
| **Sheele** | *Extase* — giant scissors that can cut through almost any material. | Gentle and clumsy in daily life, ruthless in combat. |
| **Bulat** | *Demon Armor: Incursio* — a powerful evolving armor. | Veteran swordsman, mentor to Tatsumi. |
| **Lubbock** | *Infinite Uses: Cross Tail* — near-invisible high-tensile strings. | Trapper and information broker; in love with Najenda. |
| **Chelsea** | *Gaia Foundation* — perfect disguise via cosmetic make-up. | Infiltration specialist (joins later). |
| **Susanoo** | He **is** the Teigu (a biological one). | Humanoid combat servant bound to Najenda. |

### 5.2 The Jaegers (Imperial counter-force)

Created by the Empire specifically to hunt Night Raid, commanded by the most
feared general in the army.

- **Esdeath** — Leader. Wields ***Demon's Extract***, the assimilated blood of
  an extinct ice-element Danger Beast, granting total control over ice. A
  social-Darwinist sadist who, paradoxically, falls in love with Tatsumi.
- **Wave** — Naval officer with ***Grand Chariot***, a sibling Teigu to
  Incursio. The most morally grounded Jaeger.
- **Kurome** — Akame's younger sister. Wields ***Yatsufusa***, a katana that
  turns those it kills into puppet corpses she can command.
- **Seryu Ubiquitous** — Fanatic "justice" police officer paired with a
  shapeshifting biological Teigu, ***Hekatonkheires (Koro)***, a small dog
  that transforms into a monstrous war-beast.
- **Dr. Stylish** — Sadistic surgeon with ***Perfector***, used to create
  modified human soldiers.
- **Bols** — A firefighter and family man wielding ***Rubicante***, a flame
  thrower used for "purification" missions.
- **Run** — Cultured scholar with ***Mastema***, a Teigu giving him angelic
  flight.

### 5.3 The Empire's Inner Circle

- **The Emperor** — A child manipulated by the Prime Minister.
- **Prime Minister Honest** — The story's principal villain; gluttonous,
  cunning, and the true source of the Empire's rot.
- **Budo** — Loyal general; honorable, but bound to defend the throne.
- **Various corrupt nobles** — Many of Night Raid's targets.

### 5.4 The Revolutionary Army

- Led from the south, openly at war with the Empire by the second half of the
  series. Night Raid is one of its many tools, but morally the cleanest.

## 6. Major Themes

- **No one is safe.** Death is constant and largely unsentimental.
  Heroes lose. Villains sometimes win. This was *Akame ga Kill!*'s defining
  marketing hook in 2014.
- **Justice is subjective.** Seryu and Esdeath genuinely believe they are
  serving justice. Night Raid is composed of murderers fighting for a better
  world. The series repeatedly forces both sides to confront that they kill
  people who are loved by someone.
- **Power has a cost.** Every Teigu user pays a price — physical, psychological,
  or moral.
- **Institutions vs. individuals.** Even after the Empire falls, the new order
  has to confront its own corruption. Revolution does not automatically equal
  improvement.
- **Trauma and indoctrination.** Akame and Kurome's arc, especially in
  *Akame ga Kill! Zero*, is a study in how child soldiers are made.

## 7. Anime vs. Manga: Endings Compared

Because the anime caught up with the manga, the second half of the show is an
anime-original conclusion approved by author Takahiro.

| Character | Anime Fate (2014) | Manga Fate (2016) |
|---|---|---|
| **Tatsumi** | Dies in episode 23 in his final battle against Esdeath, protecting civilians. | Survives. His body is permanently transformed by Incursio into a partly-dragon form; he returns to his village with Mine. |
| **Mine** | Dies in episode 21 of her wounds after defeating Seryu. | Falls into a long coma after defeating Budo; wakes up and eventually marries Tatsumi. |
| **Kurome** | Dies in episode 22 during her duel with Akame. | Survives the duel with Akame, recovers, and the sisters reconcile. |
| **Leone** | Dies after killing Honest in the anime's finale. | Same general beat (kills Honest), but the manga gives her death a much longer, more deliberate emotional scene. |
| **Akame** | Walks off alone into exile, framed as a tragic survivor. | Departs to neighboring nations to hunt down remaining Teigu and corrupt officials; tone is bittersweet rather than purely tragic. |

**Tone:** the anime ends bleakly, killing off most of Night Raid in quick
succession and giving Tatsumi a hero's-sacrifice ending. The manga is still a
dark series but lets more characters survive, gives the surviving cast clear
futures, and frames the post-Empire era as the *beginning* of a long, harder
fight rather than a closing tragedy.

**Why the divergence:** Takahiro has said in interviews that, because the anime
ended first, he deliberately rewrote the manga's ending so that fans who had
seen the anime would not feel they were getting the same story twice.

## 8. Reception

- The anime was a major hit in the 2014 simulcast season, particularly in the
  West, largely because of its willingness to kill off main characters
  unexpectedly. The death of **Sheele in episode 4** is often cited as the
  moment audiences realized the show was not going to play by genre rules.
- Reception of the *anime's* finale is mixed; critics generally agree the
  final arc feels rushed and that the body count became numbing rather than
  meaningful.
- The manga's ending is widely regarded as the stronger of the two: still
  dark, but emotionally earned and willing to let some hope survive.
- The franchise has remained popular in fandom for its cast design, its Teigu
  power system, and Esdeath in particular, who has become one of the most
  recognizable anime antagonists of the 2010s.

## 9. Adaptations and Spin-offs

- ***Akame ga Kill!*** — main manga, 78 chapters / 15 volumes (2010–2016).
- ***Akame ga Kill! Zero*** — prequel manga focused on Akame's Imperial
  assassin days (2013–2019).
- ***Akame ga Kill!*** anime — 24 episodes, White Fox, 2014.
- ***Hinowa ga Crush!*** — sequel/spin-off manga set in the same world,
  following a different cast in the eastern nation of Wakoku.

## 10. Quick "Why It Matters"

*Akame ga Kill!* sits at an interesting point in 2010s anime: it is one of the
clearest examples of a shōnen-style action series that openly borrows the
"anyone can die" structure from grimdark fantasy (think *Berserk*,
*Claymore*). Its main legacy is twofold — popularizing that tonal expectation
for a younger action-anime audience, and producing one of the era's most
quoted villainesses in Esdeath.
